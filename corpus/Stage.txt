   The Battlefield stage in [8]Super Smash Bros. Ultimate is an example of a typical stage in the Super Smash Bros. series.

   A stage (ステージ, Stage), also called a level, map, arena, stadium, or board, is a location in which [9]characters fight or complete objectives. The word "stage" refers to a [10]versus mode stage, but can also refer to the ground or large central [11]platform within this location. As well as versus mode stages, single player mode stages, such as the [12]Target Smash ones, exist for purposes other than fighting.

Versus stages[[34]edit]

   In all of the games of the [35]Super Smash Bros. series, most [36]versus mode stages are available from the [37]start, while a small number must be [38]unlocked by completing certain objectives. Some stages in SSB4 were made available through updates and are [39]downloadable. Of these stages, [40]only two [41]are free. Most stages, like [42]Princess Peach's Castle, are derived from places in playable characters' [43]universes. [44]Super Smash Bros. Melee introduced two [45]Super Smash Bros. universe stages — [46]Battlefield and [47]Final Destination. In [48]Super Smash Bros. Brawl, stages derived from games without associated playable characters were introduced, namely [49]Smashville and [50]Hanenbow, while [51]PictoChat represents a [52]Nintendo DS utility interface. Additionally, not every playable character has a stage from their own universe, the [53]Fire Emblem universe lacked one in Melee (though [54]one was planned), and the [55]R.O.B. universe has, for unknown reasons, never had a
   stage. Included in all games other than the first are a small number (large number in Smash Wii U and Ultimate) of [56]Past Stages — stages from a previous Smash game. These stages will have either no or minor alterations, an example being [57]Dream Land (64) in SSB4, where the top blast line is much closer to the stage then it was in 64 or Melee. Ultimate changes this, giving various stages a more refined and detailed design.

   Stages range in size from the large [58]The Great Cave Offensive and [59]Rumble Falls to the small [60]Yoshi's Story and [61]Peach's Castle. Typically, stages involve a large central [62]platform with [63]ledges, multiple smaller platforms, as well as [64]blast lines above, below, and to the left and right of the visible area. Some stages, such as [65]Mushroom Kingdom and [66]Coliseum, have floors that continue past the edge of the visible area and pass through a side blast line, these edges are known as "walk-off edges" or "walk-offs", because characters can walk offscreen without the need to become airborne. Stages with walk-off edges on both the left and right, like [67]Bridge of Eldin, are referred to as "walk-off stages", and only a select few, like [68]Yoshi's Island, have only one walk-off. Some walk-off stages, such as [69]Onett, have lower blast lines that are inaccessible normally, while others, such as [70]Green Hill Zone, only infrequently have the lower blast lines introduced;
   still others, such as [71]Mushroom Kingdom, have permanently-accessible gaps where players can fall past the lower blast line while still having solid ground covering most of the lower blast line, including its intersections with the left and/or right blast lines.

   In Melee, [72]moving and transforming stages were introduced. [73]Big Blue and [74]Rainbow Cruise consist entirely of platforms that move or appear on and offscreen, while stages like [75]Icicle Mountain and [76]PAC-LAND scroll continuously up, to the side, or down. Other stages, such as [77]Pokémon Stadium, undergo partial transformations at certain intervals, while yet others, like Brawl's [78]Castle Siege and Smash 3DS's [79]Paper Mario, cycle through complete transformations. Similarly, stages like [80]Delfino Plaza and [81]Skyloft will take players to various areas via moving platforms. [82]Mushroomy Kingdom may be one of two stages either randomly or based on a player's input prior to the match. [83]Tortimer Island's, [84]Gamer's, [85]Balloon Fight's, and [86]Garden of Hope's layouts are randomized to varying degrees for each battle as well.

   Other stage elements include breakable barriers and platforms, such as the pillars of [87]Luigi's Mansion and the stone floors of [88]Skyworld; [89]stage hazards and [90]enemies, such as lasers, cars, and [91]Klaptraps; local items such as [92]apples; and interactive objects such as [93]Barrel Cannons and switches. While [94]water has no effect on movement in Melee outside of the flowing river in [95]Jungle Japes, Brawl introduced [96]swimming, and some of its stages involve water that can be swum in.

   In Brawl and Smash 4, the appearance of some items, namely [97]crates and [98]barrels, differs depending on the aesthetic style of the stage they appear on.

   In all games, some stages (and in SSB's case, all but [99]one) are banned in [100]competitive play because they are considered disruptive or unfair. See [101]stage legality.

   Stages in [765]Super Smash Bros. 4 differ depending on the version. [766]Super Smash Bros. for Nintendo 3DS features 42 stages total, 7 of which are unlockable, shown in bold, and 8 of which are [767]downloadable content. [768]Super Smash Bros. for Wii U features 55 stages, 6 of which are unlockable, shown in bold, and 9 of which are downloadable content. In addition, this is the first game where stages from all the past entries return at once.

   All stages now have an optional [772]Ω form (Omega Form), changing their layout to be similar to Final Destination - some have walls that go all the way down to the bottom blast line, while others are basically floating islands.

   Unique to Smash 4 is the disabling of Star KOs and Screen KOs on certain stages.

In Ultimate[[1065]edit]

   The stage select for Super Smash Bros. Ultimate, showing the normal stages.
   The stage select for Super Smash Bros. Ultimate with [1066]Stage Morph enabled.

   There are 108 versus stages in [1067]Super Smash Bros. Ultimate with five being future [1068]downloadable content. Many stages return from all the previous games, although not every stage from previous titles will be returning.^[1069][1] Of the stages confirmed in Ultimate, 7 are unique to Ultimate and 96 are familiar stages: 7 from Super Smash Bros., 19 from Super Smash Bros. Melee, 26 from Super Smash Bros. Brawl, and 44 from Super Smash Bros. 4 (18 from the 3DS version, 18 from the Wii U version, and 8 that are in both versions). [1070]Ω forms return for each stage, along with a new [1071]Battlefield form for every stage. All Battlefield and Ω forms have the same size and terrain as [1072]Battlefield and [1073]Final Destination, respectively. Up to 8 players are supported on every stage.

   Not counting either single-player only stages or previous versions of Battlefield and Final Destination, there are only 15 stages that do not return in Smash Bros Ultimate ; 2 from SSB64 ([1074]Planet Zebes and [1075]Sector Z), 4 from Melee ([1076]Icicle Mountain, [1077]Mute City, [1078]Mushroom Kingdom and [1079]Poké Floats), 2 from Brawl ([1080]Rumble Falls and [1081]PictoChat), 2 from Smash for 3DS ([1082]Rainbow Road and [1083]Pac-Maze), and 5 from Smash for Wii U ([1084]Miiverse, [1085]Woolly World, [1086]Orbital Gate Assault, [1087]Jungle Hijinxs and [1088]Pyrosphere).

   A new [1089]rules option allows [1090]stage hazards to be turned off. Stages are ordered by when they first appeared in the Super Smash Bros. series. Unlike previous entries, all stages are available from the start. Ultimate also introduces [1091]Stage Morph, an option which allows players to choose two different stages which will transition back and forth over the course of a battle.

Single player stages[[1394]edit]

   In the single player modes and challenges, a number of stages appear that are not available in versus mode.
     * The [1395]Home-Run Contest stages in Melee, Brawl, and SSB4 involve a central platform from which [1396]Sandbag is hit, and a very long stretch of ground to the right of the platform for it to land upon.
     * In Melee's "trophy tussle" [1397]event matches, the player fights [1398]CPUs upon a stage that takes the form of a giant object, the trophy for which he or she wins after completing the event.
     * The original [1399]Super Smash Bros. [1400]Board the Platforms stages function as agility tests, and there are 12 in total - each one is designed to challenge the player's character.
     * Like Board the Platforms, [1401]Break the Targets and Target Test stages of the original Super Smash Bros. and Melee respectively are tailored to challenge each character - 12 exist in the original and 25 in Melee. Some of Melee's Target Test stages are decorated in reference to their character's universe; [1402]Pikachu's, for example, has decorative Poké Balls in its walls, while the [1403]Ice Climbers' look like a level from the game [1404]Ice Climber. Brawl, however, simply has five Target Smash! stages for all characters, each representing a different difficulty level.
     * Areas in [1405]Adventure Mode and the [1406]Adventure Mode: The Subspace Emissary are technically defined as stages.
     * The [1407]Online Practice Stage in Brawl, SSB4, and Ultimate is playable only while waiting for an [1408]online match to load.
     * The [1409]controls test stage in Brawl. SSB4, and Ultimate is playable only for the purpose of testing custom controls, and only as [1410]Mario.

Non-playable stages[[1470]edit]

   Some stages are inaccessible through normal means. These non-playable stages are accessible only through the use of hacks and debug programs, such as [1471]Action Replay. Some, such as "Test", were presumably used for testing during game development, while others, such as the "Tutorial stage", are used in the game but not for the purpose of gameplay.

Custom Stages[[1484]edit]

   Brawl introduced the [1485]Stage Builder, a tool that allows players to build their own stages, for use in multiplayer matches, using sets of provided objects, some unlockable. Brawl includes a set of [1486]Sample Stages that were built using the Stage Builder. The stage builder can also be used to build [1487]No KO stages and "[1488]CD Factories" - exploitative stages used for quickly obtaining [1489]CDs. The Stage Builder returns in Super Smash Bros. for Wii U, with some features removed and other features added.
